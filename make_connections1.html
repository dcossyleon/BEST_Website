You may ask: “Why is meeting people the most important part of learning about a career? Why can’t I just read about careers and find jobs online?”

The “practical” reason: 80% of jobs go to people that employers know personally or through a 3rd party. This is the “hidden job market.” Employees look to people they know first, and to an online job applicant last. Meet people to discover opportunities.

The deeper reason: The people you will meet already have the experience to tell you what skills and expertise you need to develop, and whether or not this career is a good fit for you. But they need to get to know you by meeting face-to-face. Then you can start building skills for careers that fit you, a process that takes a lot of time. Start meeting people as early as possible in your training.

—Albert Chen, PhD
Cellular and Molecular Biology Graduate Program
University of Michigan, Ann Arbor, MI
Informational Interviewing
An informational interview is a great way to reach out to someone who works in an industry that you're interested in learning more about. Think of it as an informational conversation. The goal is to learn about the person's career path, the industry, and/or the corporate culture of a potential future workplace. In turn, the person can share his or her insights and make connections for you if possible. How do I set up an informational interview?
Cold Email
  • Use your LinkedIn network, members in professional organizations, or connections from friends of friends
  • "Hi, I'm currently a graduate student at ____. I am studying ____ and am really interested in ____. I am exploring non-academic career options and I'd love to hear more about what you do. If you are not too busy, I hope we can find a time to chat, via phone or in person."
  • 20-minute phone call or a 30-minute meeting is a reasonable request
  • Mention how you found them: If connected through someone you know- mention it!
  • Keep the email short
  • Email subject is important: Include who you are & what you do
    • Emory PhD student requesting information
    • Request for career informational interview
    • Meeting request: Referred by [mutual acquaintance] for career advice
Professional Associations & Societies
Professional societies are not just for conferences and newsletters. Once you've joined and paid your dues, you have access to the member directory, job boards, professional development services, leadership opportunities within the organization, and ability to apply for special awards like travel grants. You can leverage them in your career development by taking advantage of:
  • Networking opportunities
  • Job boards and professional development services
  • Leadership and volunteer experiences

Georgia's Bioscience Industry
Georgia Bio is Georgia’s Trade Organization: “Georgia Bio's mission is to advance the growth of Georgia’s life sciences industry and foster strategic partnerships that can create a healthier world. To accomplish this mission, GaBio conducts business and economic development activities; advocates on behalf of the industry on public policy matters; educates the public about the benefits of life sciences research and product development; and provides a network for the exchange of ideas, information and opportunities.”
